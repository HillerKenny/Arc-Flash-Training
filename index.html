Module 01

What an Arc Flash Actually Is

An arc flash is a release of energy caused by an electric arc — current leaving its intended path and traveling through air between conductors, or between a conductor and ground. In an MCC bucket or e-house lineup, that gap can be closed by a dropped tool, a loose connection, dust and moisture buildup, rodent damage, or a slip of a meter probe.

The arc itself burns at up to 35,000°F — roughly four times hotter than the surface of the sun. It produces:

  • Intense heat capable of vaporizing copper and causing third-degree burns at several feet
  • An arc blast — a pressure wave that can exceed hundreds of pounds per square foot, throwing people and equipment doors
  • Molten metal spray and shrapnel from vaporized conductors and enclosure components
  • Blinding light and sound exceeding 140 decibels — equivalent to a gunshot at close range
Why MCCs & E-Houses specifically Motor control centers and e-houses concentrate many breakers, starters, and bus connections in a small footprint, often at higher fault currents than a typical panelboard. Bucket pull-out/in operations, racking breakers, and meter testing inside these enclosures are among the highest-frequency arc flash exposure tasks in industrial and life-safety facilities.
A note for mine sites MSHA, not OSHA, has jurisdiction over electrical safety at mine sites (30 CFR Part 56/57, Subpart K), and MSHA's own regulations have not formally adopted NFPA 70E the way most general industry has. That said, many mine operators — including Freeport-McMoRan — require NFPA 70E compliance as part of their site electrical safety program, even though it isn't an MSHA mandate. At Freeport-McMoRan specifically, completing this training is what qualifies you to enter MCC rooms and e-houses at all — a site electrician opens the space and briefs you on anything specific to that location before you begin fire alarm or life-safety work. As a fire protection professional, the general rule is to stay out of energized areas above 60V and ask the site electrician to de-energize when possible — this training covers hazard awareness, not electrical work authorization. In a recent 6-month MSHA reporting period, 11 miners were injured by electric arc flashes — including incidents during routine breaker box maintenance, the exact kind of task fire/life-safety techs often need access near.

Module 02

Do's and Don'ts: MCCs & E-Houses

These are the field-level rules. They don't replace the site-specific briefing you'll get from a site electrician or NFPA 70E hazard/risk assessment — they're the baseline behaviors that keep you out of the incident report. There's a question after the Do's and another after the Don'ts — read each list carefully.

Do

  • Verify the arc flash label and PPE category on the bucket/cabinet before opening it
  • De-energize, lock out/tag out, and verify zero energy state whenever the task allows it
  • Treat every MCC bucket as energized until proven otherwise with a rated meter
  • Wear the PPE category called for on the equipment label, every time, even for "quick looks"
  • Keep the e-house door open and an escape path clear before energized work
  • Use insulated, rated tools and verify meter leads/CAT rating before testing
  • Stand clear of the bucket's hinge line and away from the direct line of an open door when racking breakers
  • Have a second qualified person aware of the task when working inside an energized lineup
  • Report loose connections, discoloration, heat damage, or unusual odors immediately

Best Practices — MSHA Required Checklist

  • Establish safe procedures before beginning work and discuss the plan with all miners involved in the task.
  • Electricians must know how to de-energize and disconnect electrical systems and equipment.
  • Use properly rated electrical meters and personal protective equipment, such as electrically rated gloves, insulated blankets or mats, and polycarbonate barriers.
  • Lock out and tag out circuits before working on electrical equipment:
    • Identify and control all hazardous energy sources before conducting any task.
    • Open the circuit breaker or load break switch to de-energize the incoming power cables or conductors.
    • Open the visual disconnect to confirm incoming power is off.
    • Lockout/Tagout the visual disconnect.
    • Ground the de-energized conductors.

Module 04

Incident Energy, cal/cm², & PPE Categories

What Does "cal" Mean?

The "cal" in cal/cm² stands for calorie — a unit of thermal (heat) energy. In everyday life, you know calories as a food energy measurement, but in physics a calorie is simply the amount of heat required to raise one gram of water by one degree Celsius. In arc flash safety, a calorie is used to measure how much heat energy reaches a worker's body during an arc flash event.

The full unit — cal/cm² — is "calories per square centimeter," meaning the amount of heat energy landing on each square centimeter of a person's body at a given distance from the arc. The higher the number, the more heat per square centimeter, and the more severe the potential burn.

The key reference point: 1.2 cal/cm² At 1.2 cal/cm², unprotected skin will sustain a second-degree burn — blistering, pain, and potential scarring. This is not a catastrophic amount of energy in absolute terms: one useful mental image is holding your hand one inch over the flame of a cigarette lighter for one second, which delivers roughly 1 cal/cm². The arc flash boundary on every label you'll see in an MCC room is calculated at exactly this threshold — the point where bare skin begins to be seriously injured.

How Incident Energy Is Calculated

Incident energy — the number on your arc flash label in cal/cm² — is calculated by electrical engineers using IEEE Standard 1584, based on real arc flash tests across hundreds of equipment configurations. Three factors drive the result more than anything else:

  • Available fault current: How much current can flow through the arc. Higher fault current means more energy — but not always more incident energy at the label, because circuit breakers clear faster at higher currents.
  • Arc duration (clearing time): How long the arc burns before the upstream breaker or fuse trips it off. This is the single biggest driver of incident energy — time is more critical than current. An arc lasting 0.5 seconds releases 10× more energy than one lasting 0.05 seconds.
  • Working distance: How far the worker's face and chest are from the arc. Incident energy drops with the square of distance — double the distance, and the incident energy drops to roughly one quarter. This is why your working distance matters and why standing to the side of an open bucket matters.

The result of that calculation is placed on the arc flash label on each piece of equipment, along with the arc flash boundary distance, the PPE category or minimum cal/cm² rating required, and the working distance the calculation assumed. Always check the label — never assume.

Why time is the most dangerous variable A 480V MCC bucket at a facility with slow-clearing breakers can easily reach 20–40 cal/cm² of incident energy. The same physical bucket at a facility with fast arc flash relay protection might show only 2–5 cal/cm². The voltage is the same. The difference is entirely how long the breaker takes to clear. This is why a label from a different facility or a generic "480V = low hazard" assumption can get someone killed — the incident energy must be calculated for that specific installation.

PPE Categories and What They Require

The four NFPA 70E PPE Categories define the minimum arc-rated equipment required based on the calculated incident energy. The category number on an arc flash label tells you the minimum category of PPE required — or the label may list the incident energy directly in cal/cm², in which case your PPE must be rated at or above that number.

PPE CategoryMinimum Arc RatingRequired Gear (minimum)
Category 14 cal/cm²Arc-rated shirt + pants or coverall (4 cal/cm² min); arc-rated face shield or hood; safety glasses; heavy-duty leather gloves; hard hat; hearing protection
Category 28 cal/cm²Arc-rated shirt + pants or coverall (8 cal/cm² min); arc-rated face shield with balaclava OR arc flash hood; rubber-insulating gloves with leather protectors; hard hat; hearing protection. Most common for 480V MCC work.
Category 325 cal/cm²Arc flash suit jacket + pants or coverall (25 cal/cm² min); arc flash suit hood; rubber-insulating gloves with leather protectors; hard hat; hearing protection; safety glasses
Category 440 cal/cm²Arc flash suit jacket + pants or coverall (40 cal/cm² min); arc flash suit hood; rubber-insulating gloves with leather protectors; hard hat; hearing protection; safety glasses
Arc Thermal Performance Value (ATPV) The rating on arc-rated clothing — the "4 cal/cm²" or "8 cal/cm²" number on the tag — is called the ATPV (Arc Thermal Performance Value). It represents the incident energy level at which the garment has a 50% probability of preventing a second-degree burn. This means your PPE rating must always meet or exceed the incident energy shown on the label — never fall below it. A 4 cal/cm² shirt does not protect you from a 6 cal/cm² arc flash.

The actual numbers — NFPA 70E Table 130.4(E)(a) For typical MCC/e-house voltages (151V–750V, which covers standard 208V, 277V, and 480V systems): the Limited Approach Boundary is 3 feet 6 inches from a fixed part (10 feet from a movable/exposed conductor), and the Restricted Approach Boundary is 1 foot 0 inches. That restricted distance is closer than it sounds — a worker standing in front of an open bucket reaching toward a terminal is often already inside it. These numbers increase at higher voltages and are listed on your site's arc flash study; treat the figures above as the baseline for standard low-voltage MCC equipment, not a substitute for the equipment-specific label.

Module 05

Entering MCC Rooms & E-Houses at Freeport-McMoRan

At Freeport-McMoRan sites, completing this arc flash and NFPA 70E awareness training is what qualifies you to enter an MCC room or e-house in the first place — without that training, you are not permitted in these spaces at all, even with a site electrician present. This module covers exactly how that access works once you're trained, so you know what to expect and what's expected of you.

How entry actually works here Once you've completed this training, a site electrician opens the room or enclosure for you and gives you a site-specific briefing before you start work — covering things like higher voltage at a substation, unusual equipment in that specific room, or any other hazard particular to that location. After that briefing, you can work on your own fire alarm and life-safety tasks in that space. The general rule of thumb: fire protection professionals do not service or work on energized equipment above 60V — that work belongs to qualified site electricians. If your task requires entering an area with exposed energized conductors above 60V, ask the electrician to de-energize the area first whenever possible. This training gets the door opened and the hazard briefing done — it does not make you an electrician.

Before You Can Enter

  • Complete this arc flash / NFPA 70E awareness training — this is the baseline requirement for entry at all
  • Have a site electrician open the MCC room, e-house, or electrical room for you — you don't open these yourself
  • If energized equipment above 60V is exposed in your work area, ask the electrician to de-energize it before you start
  • Get the site-specific hazard briefing for that exact room before starting work (voltage level, layout quirks, anything unusual to that location)
  • Confirm you understand the briefing — ask questions before the electrician leaves, not after

What This Doesn't Mean

  • This training does not make you a qualified electrical worker — you do not open buckets, rack breakers, or service energized equipment above 60V
  • Don't enter an MCC room or e-house without this training completed, even briefly, even for something that feels routine
  • Don't assume "I'm just installing a detector" means you don't need to coordinate with the electrician about the energized equipment around you
  • Don't skip the site-specific briefing because you've been in a similar room before — voltage and layout can differ room to room
